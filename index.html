Basic Skills Model of Literacy

A critical examination of autonomous literacy models from Street's perspective

The autonomous model treats literacy as mechanical skills acquisition

Defining the Basic Skills Model

According to Brian Street's framework, the Basic Skills Model represents what he terms an autonomous model of literacy - one that views literacy as a neutral, technical skill divorced from social, cultural, and political contexts.

  • Treats literacy as a set of decontextualized, individual cognitive skills
  • Focuses primarily on surface language features: grammar, spelling, punctuation
  • Assumes skills can be transferred unproblematically across different contexts
  • Rooted in behaviorist and transmission models of learning
  • Often employs a "deficit" approach, particularly targeting marginalized student populations
"Autonomous models of literacy are based on essay-text forms of literacy rooted in Western academic circles and represents a culturally specific model that is masked in claims of universalism." - Street (1995), Social Literacies

Political Implications

Street's analysis reveals how the Basic Skills Model serves specific political purposes by maintaining existing power structures:

  • Presents Western academic literacy as a universal standard
  • Marginalizes alternative forms of literate knowledge and expression
  • Reinforces social hierarchies by positioning certain students as "deficient"
  • Used as a tool for social control within educational institutions
  • Often targets African-American, Latino, and other marginalized students for "remedial" instruction
"If literacy is represented as a context-neutral skill, then it fulfills the political purposes of those in power to maintain a position of superiority by marginalizing other forms of literate knowledge." - Larson (1996), Challenging Autonomous Models of Literacy

Pedagogical Consequences

The implementation of Basic Skills approaches has significant consequences for teaching and learning:

  • Reduces literacy to mechanical exercises divorced from meaningful communication
  • Prioritizes standardized testing and measurable outcomes over authentic learning
  • Creates artificial dichotomies (e.g., Whole Language vs. Phonics debates)
  • Limits student engagement and critical thinking about texts
  • Undermines teacher professionalism by prescribing "one-size-fits-all" approaches
"Through the 'pedagogization of literacy,' objectified conceptions of literacy are naturalized in practice... literacy is defined apart from social context and becomes a 'content to be taught through authority structures.'" - Street & Street (1995), The Schooling of Literacy

Moving Beyond the Basic Skills Model

Street advocates for a shift toward an ideological model of literacy that recognizes:

  • Literacy as socially situated practice rather than neutral skill
  • The importance of power relations in literacy practices
  • Multiple literacies that vary across cultures and contexts
  • The role of identity and agency in literacy development
  • Need for critical approaches that make power relationships explicit
"In Street's ideological model, literacy is conceptualized as a critical social practice that makes explicit underlying assumptions and power relations inherent in conceptions of literacy as social process." - Larson (1996), Challenging Autonomous Models of Literacy
